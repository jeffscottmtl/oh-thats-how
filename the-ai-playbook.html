I
Start here

The Four Questions.

Every prompt in this library follows the same framework. Answer these four questions before you hit Enter, and 80% of bad AI output disappears.

1

What job am I asking AI to do?

Pick the job: draft, edit, summarize, translate, brainstorm. Set the focus. Set the red lines, what NOT to mention.

e.g. "Draft an internal message. Do not speculate on cause or restoration date."
2

Who is it for, and what should it achieve?

Name the audience. Set the tone. State the desired outcome, what you want the reader to do, feel, or decide.

e.g. "For unionized employees. Direct, respectful tone. Build understanding, not speculation."
3

What format should it use?

Specify the deliverable. Set the length. Set the structure, headers, bullets, word counts, order of information.

e.g. "Email. 150–180 words. Subject line, opening, three bullets, closing."
4

What info & rules does AI need?

Give it the facts, background, and reference examples. State house rules: Canadian spelling, no acronyms, no em dashes, placeholders for missing facts.

e.g. "Change takes effect April 15, Montreal yard only. Use Canadian spelling."

Head to the Learn tab for the full explainer and worked example.

II
Know who you're writing for

Six CN audiences, six tones.

The same message hits differently depending on who's reading it. Name the audience in your prompt, every time.

AUDIENCE 01
Executives

Concise, decision-oriented. Numbers up front. No preamble.

AUDIENCE 02
Management employees

Context + direction. What changed, what to do.

AUDIENCE 03
Front-line supervisors (FLS)

Practical, operational. Talking points they can use.

AUDIENCE 04
Unionized employees

Direct, respectful, clear. No corporate softening.

AUDIENCE 05
Media & public

News-first. Facts, quotes, context. Every word defensible.

AUDIENCE 06
Investors

Precise. Cite sources. Nothing market-sensitive pre-disclosure.

Guides / Guide 01
Writing · Most used · 4 min read

Draft an email in 90 seconds.

The workhorse guide. A prompt pattern that produces a clear, appropriately-toned email on the first try, and the five edits you should always make before hitting send.

Audience: anyone Tool: any chat AI Time: 2 min prompt · 1 min edit
01

Use AI for this. Don't for that.

The first decision

AI is a first-draft engine, not a judgment engine. Use it for volume, structure, and tone. Don't outsource the decisions.

✓ Great for
  • Status updates, FYIs, chasers
  • Meeting requests and intros
  • Translating bullet points into prose
  • Softening or firming a tone
  • Writing in a language you're rusty in
  • "Say this nicely" rewrites
✗ Stop and write it yourself
  • Condolences or sympathy
  • Performance or HR conversations
  • Legally binding commitments
  • Anything involving a specific person's confidential situation
  • Apologies on behalf of the org, get them reviewed
02

The prompt pattern.

Copy, adapt, paste

Fill in the italic bits. Keep the structure. This pattern works in Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, or Gemini.

You are helping me draft an email. // Context I am [your role] at [org]. I am writing to [recipient, name, role, how well you know them]. Our relationship: [first contact / ongoing / tense / warm]. // Goal The single thing this email needs to achieve: [the ONE outcome]. // Inputs Here is what I want to say, in rough bullets: - [point 1] - [point 2] - [ask] // Constraints - Tone: [warm / professional / diplomatic-firm / apologetic] - Length: under [120] words - Do NOT use: "I hope this finds you well", "circle back", "touch base", exclamation marks - Match this voice sample from my past writing: [paste 2-3 of your previous emails] // Output Give me: 1. A subject line (under 8 words, specific) 2. The email body 3. One alternate version with a firmer tone Then stop. Do not add commentary.
Why the voice sample matters

Without it, every email comes out sounding like a LinkedIn post. Two or three of your past emails teach the model your rhythm, your sign-offs, your hedges. This single addition is the difference between usable and uncanny.

03

Before & after.

Vague prompt vs. the pattern

✗ Vague prompt

"Write an email to our MP about the bridge project."

Output (generic)

Dear Honourable Member,

I hope this email finds you well. I am writing to you today regarding the ongoing bridge project in our community. As you may be aware, this project represents a significant investment...

✓ Pattern prompt

Role, goal, bullets, tone "diplomatic-firm", 120 words, voice sample attached.

Output (usable first draft)

Subject: Bridge project, timing question before Thursday

Priya,

A quick note ahead of Thursday's constituency meeting. We've had three delivery dates slip since January, and residents on the east side are asking pointed questions I can't keep fielding without an update from your office.

Could you confirm by Wednesday whether the June window still holds? If it doesn't, I'd rather know now so we can manage expectations together.

Happy to jump on a call.

-- Sam

04

Four variants to save.

For the situations you hit weekly

a. The "I disagree, politely"

When you need to push back without burning the relationship.

Rewrite this email so it: - Clearly registers that I disagree with [specific point] - Keeps the door open for next steps - Avoids passive-aggressive phrasing ("as previously discussed", "per my last email") - Ends with a concrete proposal, not a complaint Original: [paste]

b. The chaser

Second or third follow-up. The trick: acknowledge the silence rather than ignoring it.

Draft a follow-up email. This is my [second / third] chase. Rules: - Under 60 words - Acknowledge the silence briefly, without guilt-tripping - Restate the ask in one sentence - Offer an out ("if this has been deprioritised, just tell me") - No "gentle reminder", no "per my last email" Thread so far: [paste]

c. The stakeholder update

Monthly/quarterly note to a list. The risk: sounding like a newsletter no one asked for.

Write a stakeholder update email based on these bullets: [paste] Format: - Subject: what changed, not "Monthly update" - Opening line: the one thing they need to know - 3 bullet points, max 15 words each - One clear ask or next step - Sign-off: my name, nothing else Tone: peer-to-peer, not broadcast. Length: under 150 words. Cut ruthlessly.

d. The "thanks, but no"

Declining an invitation, request, or proposal without closing the door.

Draft a polite decline. Context: [who is asking, what they want, why I am saying no, our relationship]. Structure: 1. Thank them specifically (name the thing) 2. Say no clearly, no euphemisms ("unfortunately not" is fine; "at this time we are unable to accommodate" is not) 3. Give one honest reason if I have one; otherwise don't invent one 4. Offer something small if possible: a referral, a future window, an introduction 5. Close warmly Under 100 words.
05

The five-edit rule.

Before you hit send

Never send AI's first draft. Make these five edits, every time.

  1. Cut the opening pleasantry.

    "I hope this email finds you well" is AI's calling card. Delete it. Start with the actual point, or a one-line human opener ("Quick one before Thursday,").

  2. Verify every name, number, and date.

    AI makes up plausible specifics. If it says "the 2023 report found 62%," you haven't read that report, find it or remove the stat.

  3. Read it aloud. Cut anything you wouldn't say.

    "Leverage", "synergies", "circle back", "at this juncture", if you wouldn't say it to their face, cut it.

  4. Check the ask is in the first 3 sentences.

    AI buries the ask in the third paragraph. Move it up. Busy people skim.

  5. Add one specific, human touch.

    A reference to their last message. A remark about context only you two share. One sentence of you. This is the thing that keeps AI emails from feeling like AI emails.

06

Red-flag phrases.

Ctrl-F these before sending

If any of these survive in your draft, you sent AI's voice, not yours.

• "I hope this finds you well"
• "I wanted to reach out"
• "Please don't hesitate to"
• "In today's fast-paced world"
• "Leverage / synergies / align"
• "Delve into / dive deep"
• "It's worth noting that"
• "In conclusion,"
• "I would like to take this opportunity"
• "Moving forward,"
• "Crucial / vital / pivotal / robust"
• Any phrase that sounds corporate and hollow
Habit

Keep a "red-flag list" in a note. Every time you catch a new AI-ism in your own drafts, add it. Within a month you'll spot them in two seconds.

Guides / Guide 02
Editing & review · 3 min read

Proofread without losing your voice.

The trap: you ask AI to "polish" your draft and it hands back something that reads like a trade-journal editorial. Here's how to ask for corrections, not a rewrite.

Tool: any chat AI Time: 2 min
01

Three levels of review.

Ask for the right one

AI will default to the heaviest edit possible. Your job is to ask for the lightest one that solves your problem.

L1Copy-edit only.
Typos, grammar, punctuation. Zero word changes otherwise. Use when you trust your own writing.
L2Line-edit.
L1 plus clunky phrasing, repetition, vague words. Preserves voice. Use on first drafts.
L3Structural.
Reorder paragraphs, cut sections, rewrite the opening. Use only when you know the draft is broken.
02

The prompt.

For L1 + L2, the ones you'll use 90% of the time

Proofread the text below. Do a Level 2 edit: CHANGE: - Typos, grammar, punctuation - Awkward or repetitive phrasing - Vague words where a specific one exists - Sentences that run longer than 30 words DO NOT CHANGE: - My vocabulary or sentence rhythm - Contractions (keep "don't", "we'll") - British / Canadian / Australian / American spelling, match what's already there - Anything that works, just to show you did something Return the edit as a tracked-changes list: → [original phrase] becomes [new phrase] -- because [reason] Do not return a rewritten document. I want to approve each change myself. Text: [paste]
The key move

Asking for a tracked-changes list instead of a rewritten draft changes everything. You see each suggestion, accept what works, reject the rest. You stay the author.

03

Before & after.

Voice intact

✗ "Polish this" (L3 by default)

Original: "The meeting went sideways fast. We had three asks, got none of them, and left with a new problem."

After: "The meeting did not proceed as anticipated. Despite presenting three distinct requests, we were unable to secure agreement on any of them, and we departed with an additional challenge to address."

Your voice: gone

✓ "L2 tracked changes"

Original: "The meeting went sideways fast. We had three asks, got none of them, and left with a new problem."

Suggested: → "sideways fast" works, keep
→ "got none of them" → "got none agreed" (clearer)
→ "a new problem" → "a fourth issue to handle" (specific)

Your voice: intact

04

Two special cases.

When L2 isn't enough

a. "Read this as my sharpest critic would."

Before sending something high-stakes, a board paper, a public statement, a letter to a regulator.

Read the text below as the most skeptical version of my audience: [e.g., a hostile journalist / an opposition MP / a regulator looking for gaps]. Give me: 1. Three sentences they would quote out of context to make us look bad. Quote them back to me. 2. Two claims they would demand evidence for. 3. One thing that's technically defensible but reads badly. 4. The single weakest paragraph. Do not rewrite anything. Just identify the risks. Text: [paste]

b. "Is this actually clear?"

For anything jargon-heavy, policy, technical briefs, legal-adjacent writing.

Read the text below and tell me: 1. What is this document actually asking the reader to do or believe? State it in one sentence, using the simplest possible words. 2. Which paragraphs, if removed, would NOT hurt that goal? 3. Which three sentences are the hardest to understand? Quote them back. 4. Is there a moment where the reader would reasonably get lost? Where? Do not rewrite. Just diagnose. Text: [paste]
05

Things AI should not touch.

Protect these in your prompt

  • Quoted speech. If you're quoting someone, AI will "tidy" the quote. Add: "Do not modify anything between quotation marks."
  • Names and titles. Add: "Do not change any proper nouns, titles, or honorifics."
  • Legal phrasing. "Without prejudice", "on a confidential basis", specific regulatory language, flag them in the prompt as do-not-touch.
  • Deliberate repetition. If you repeated a word for rhetorical effect, AI will "fix" it. Tell it the repetition is intentional.
  • Technical terms your audience expects. "Stakeholder capitalism", "material change", "in-camera", add a glossary of terms to preserve.
Guides / Guide 03
Writing · 6 min read · 4 templates

From bullets
to a speech you can actually say.

Written words and spoken words have different physics. AI knows written. Your job is to pull it toward spoken, short sentences, hard stops, and sentences your principal wouldn't stumble over.

Audience: writers, speechwriters, EAs Time: 10–30 min
01

Start with structure.

Pick a shape before you write a word

Most bad speeches fail at the outline, not the sentences. Ask AI for three outline options, pick one, then draft.

I need a speech outline. Give me THREE different structural options before I pick one. CONTEXT - Speaker: [name, role, speaking style if known] - Audience: [who, how many, what they care about, what they're sceptical of] - Occasion: [event, time of day, tone expected] - Length: [minutes] -- roughly [words] at speaking pace - The ONE thing the audience should remember tomorrow: [message] For each of the 3 options, give me: - The structural logic in one sentence (e.g. "problem → pivot → promise") - An opener (first 2 sentences, ready to speak) - 4-6 section headers with one line each - A closer (last 2 sentences) Do NOT draft the whole speech yet. Just the three outlines.
Why three

One outline is a suggestion; three is a choice. Seeing three side-by-side tells you what each structure costs, and usually makes the right one obvious.

02

Draft for the ear.

Different rules from writing for the eye

Once you've picked an outline, draft one section at a time. Long prompts get flabby outputs; short ones stay tight.

Draft the [section name] section of a speech for [speaker]. SPOKEN-WORD RULES (hard): - Average sentence: under 14 words. Cap at 22. - No semicolons. No dashes used as pauses. Use a full stop. - No words the speaker would pause before: "notwithstanding", "concomitant", "paradigm" - Read aloud test: if it needs a breath mid-sentence, cut it - Contractions: yes ("we're", "won't") - No lists of more than three. Three beats. Always three. - Repetition is fine. Repetition lands. Repetition is how speeches work. CONTENT TO WORK FROM [paste bullets, quotes, stats] Draft ~[N] words. Format with line breaks between paragraphs. At the end, flag any stat or quote I need to verify.
03

The opener & the closer.

Spend disproportionate time here

The first 30 seconds and the last 30 seconds are 80% of what the room remembers. Worth 10 prompts, not one.

Opener generator

Give me FIVE different openers for a speech by [speaker] to [audience] about [topic]. Try five different shapes: 1. A specific moment (a scene, a date, a person) 2. A direct acknowledgment of the room (why we're here, who's missing) 3. A surprising number or fact 4. A question the audience is already asking themselves 5. A short, honest admission Each opener: 2-3 sentences, under 40 words. No "Good morning and thank you for having me."

Closer generator

Give me THREE closers for the speech below. The audience should leave with one clear feeling and one clear action. One feeling: [e.g., urgency / pride / clarity] One action: [e.g., write to their MP / read the report / show up Thursday] Rules: - Under 60 words each - Last sentence must be speakable in one breath - No "in conclusion", "thank you for your time", "I'll now take questions" - Return to an image or phrase from the opener if one fits, but only if it fits Speech draft: [paste]
04

Anticipate the Q&A.

Before the speech, not after

If the speech will be followed by questions, and most are, rehearse them before you write the closer.

Act as three different people in the audience for this speech. For each, give me: - The single hardest question they would ask - Why it's hard (what assumption or weakness it probes) - A 40-word answer the speaker could actually give Three people: 1. A friendly journalist looking for a headline 2. A sceptic in the room who disagrees with the premise 3. A bureaucrat who wants to know about cost and timing Speech: [paste]
05

Pre-delivery checklist.

Final pass

  • Read it aloud. Not in your head. Out loud. Time it.
  • Mark the breath points. Slashes where the speaker pauses. If a sentence has no breath, rewrite it.
  • Cut 10% of words. On stage, every extra word is a drag. This is the single highest-leverage edit.
  • Verify every number, name, date, quote. A speech gets clipped on social media. One wrong stat lives forever.
  • Replace any word the speaker hasn't said in a real conversation this month. If it's not in their vocabulary, it won't sound like them.
  • Sound check for acronyms. Spell them out the first time. Don't make the audience decode.
  • Print it in the speaker's preferred format. Double-spaced, 14pt, bold the emphasis words. Paper, not phone.
Guides / Guide 04
Stakeholder relations · 5 min read

Letters: formal, firm, & human.

Letters are the slowest, most considered form of business correspondence. Tone is everything. Here's how to move a letter up and down the tone ladder without losing the point.

01

The tone ladder.

Same message, five registers

Before you prompt, decide which rung. Most "letter" prompts default to rung 3, which is neither warm enough for a constituent nor firm enough for a regulator.

Rung 1
"Warmly personal." Thank-you letters, condolences (only if hand-edited), invitations to people you know.
Rung 2
"Considerate official." Constituent replies, responses to public inquiries, appreciative stakeholder notes.
Rung 3
"Neutral official." Standard correspondence, confirmations, procedural notices.
Rung 4
"Diplomatic-firm." Disagreements, escalations, "your proposal is insufficient" letters.
Rung 5
"Formal-terse." Regulator-bound, legally-adjacent, pre-litigation. Run past counsel.
02

The prompt.

Name the rung explicitly

Draft a formal letter. TONE RUNG: [1-5, see the ladder] -- If Rung 1/2: warm opening, acknowledge the person specifically -- If Rung 3: neutral, procedural, no sentiment -- If Rung 4: firm but not rude; register disagreement clearly; keep the door open -- If Rung 5: minimal; facts and positions only; no concessions beyond what I specify FROM: [your name, title, org] TO: [recipient name, title, org, full postal address] DATE: [today] RE: [subject line, specific] KEY POINTS to include (in this order if possible): 1. [point] 2. [point] 3. [explicit ask OR position] CONSTRAINTS - Length: under [one page / 400 words] - No clichés ("I hope this letter finds you well") - British / Canadian / US spelling: [pick] - If Rung 4/5: every claim must be directly supported by what I've given you. Do not infer or soften. Return the full letter including salutation, body, sign-off, and a placeholder line for signature.
03

Constituent & public replies.

Scale without feeling form-lettered

The trick: AI writes the body; you hand-write two sentences acknowledging their specific case.

Draft a reply to a constituent/member of the public. WHAT THEY WROTE (verbatim): [paste] OUR POSITION: [paste the policy/position note] RULES: - Rung 2 tone: considerate official - Start by naming the specific concern THEY raised (not a generic "thank you for writing about [topic]") - Address their ask directly, yes, no, partially, not yet, or "here's who can help" - If we can't do what they want, say why in one honest sentence. Not three paragraphs of process. - Close with one tangible next step (a link, a name, a date) - No "please don't hesitate to contact us", say how they can follow up specifically Leave a [INSERT PERSONAL NOTE HERE] placeholder before the sign-off for me to add 2 sentences by hand.
Never do this

Don't paste constituent data (names, addresses, case numbers, sensitive details) into a consumer AI tool. Redact to "[CONSTITUENT NAME]" and "[ADDRESS]", or use an approved enterprise tool.

04

The "diplomatic-firm" letter.

Rung 4, the hardest rung

The letter that records a disagreement, formally, without closing the relationship. AI tends to over-soften. You want clarity without contempt.

Draft a Rung-4 diplomatic-firm letter. SITUATION: [background] WHAT THEY DID / PROPOSED: [specifics] WHY IT'S UNACCEPTABLE (internal note, do NOT include verbatim): [candid] WHAT WE WANT INSTEAD: [specific ask] DEADLINE / NEXT STEP: [date and consequence] TONE RULES - Register the disagreement in the first paragraph, unambiguously. Do NOT bury it. - One paragraph of reasoning, fact-based, not emotional - The ask must be specific and time-bound - No veiled threats; state consequences plainly and only if I have authority to deliver them - Close courteously. "We look forward to your response by [date]." No warmth past that. Produce two versions: A) The one I asked for B) One clicks firmer (Rung 4.5), in case legal wants it pointier
05

Pre-mail checklist.

A letter goes on the record

  • Address correct? Name, title, honorific, organisation. AI gets these wrong constantly.
  • Dates and deadlines. Every date verified; every deadline achievable.
  • Position matches our actual position. Not AI's softened version of it.
  • Letters FOI/disclosure-safe. Would you be comfortable if this letter were made public tomorrow?
  • Signed off by the right person. AI doesn't know your delegation limits. You do.
  • Copied to the right people. CC line matches the politics.
  • Legal review if Rung 5. Non-negotiable.
Guides / Guide 05
Internal comms · 5 min read

30-page report to a 10-slide story.

AI is brilliant for the outline. Mediocre for the slides themselves. The trick is to stop at the right moment and take over.

01

Outline first. Always.

Never ask for slides straight away

If you ask AI "make me a 10-slide deck", you get ten generic slides. If you ask for an outline, you get a spine you can defend.

I have source material for a presentation. BEFORE drafting slides, build me an outline. AUDIENCE: [who, what they already know, what they want from this meeting] LENGTH: [N] slides, [M] minutes ONE-SENTENCE GOAL: at the end of this meeting, the audience will [do / decide / understand]. SOURCE: [paste the report / briefing / bullets] Give me: 1. A one-sentence thesis for the whole deck 2. A slide-by-slide outline, for each slide: · a short title (under 8 words, stating the POINT, not the topic) · the single sentence that slide must prove · 1-3 supporting facts from the source 3. A list of anything in the source that I am NOT using, and why 4. The single slide the audience will remember, which one? why? Do not draft the slides themselves yet.
The "point, not topic" rule

"Q3 Results" is a topic. "Revenue grew 14% despite policy headwinds" is a point. Audiences remember points. They forget topics. Force this in every slide title.

02

Draft one slide at a time.

Resist batch mode

Once you've approved the outline, ask for one slide's content at a time. Slower, but the output is usable.

Draft the content for slide [N]. TITLE (from the outline): [paste] THIS SLIDE MUST PROVE: [the sentence] Give me: - A tightened version of the title (under 8 words, active verb if possible) - ONE big-number or big-quote I can place visually prominent, if the source supports one - 3 bullet points, max 12 words each, but only if bullets genuinely help. If a single sentence is stronger, return one sentence. - Speaker notes: ~50 words of what the presenter would say while this slide is up. Conversational, not scripted. - A note on what NOT to put on this slide (things the presenter should say out loud instead) Source for this slide: [paste the relevant section]
03

The density audit.

AI overfills slides. Trim.

Run this at the end, once the full deck exists.

Audit this slide deck for density. Go slide by slide. For each: 1. Count words on the slide (headline + bullets + labels, not speaker notes) 2. Flag any slide over 40 words as overloaded 3. Identify the weakest word on each slide, the one that adds the least 4. Identify any slide whose title is a topic instead of a point, rewrite as a point 5. Spot duplicate concepts across slides, two slides making the same argument Return a table with columns: slide #, word count, verdict (lean / okay / overloaded), one specific cut to make. Deck: [paste]
04

Visual suggestions.

What to put next to the words

AI can't design your slide. It can tell you what kind of image or chart each slide wants.

For each slide in this deck, suggest the visual. Pick from: - Big number (one huge figure, one label) - Chart (say what kind, what it shows, what shape it should have if the data supports the argument) - Photograph (say who/what is in it; a specific scene, not "people collaborating") - Pull quote (say who said it and why it belongs on this slide) - Diagram (describe the shape in 1 sentence) - Nothing, words only Rule: if a slide doesn't have a clear visual idea, that's a sign the slide doesn't have a clear point. Flag those. Deck: [paste]
05

Pre-show checklist.

Before you share screen

  • Every slide title is a point, not a topic.
  • Every number on a slide has a source. You know where each came from.
  • No slide over 40 words. If it is, split or cut.
  • You can present without the slides. If the slides disappeared, you could still make the argument. If you can't, you don't understand it yet.
  • Speaker notes read like speech. If they read like a memo, rewrite.
  • A backup slide exists for the hostile question. You know which question and where the slide is.
  • Final slide is an ask. Not "thank you". What you want the audience to do.
Guides / Guide 06
Media · 4 min read

Q&A prep & media statements.

Surface the hostile questions before the journalist does. Draft a 40-word statement you can actually defend.

For: Media Relations Tool: ChatGPT Time: 15 min
01

Use AI for this. Don't for that.

Know the line before you start

Great for
  • Generating 20+ hostile questions in 60 seconds
  • Role-playing a journalist in back-and-forth
  • Drafting tight 40-word holding statements
  • Identifying weak spots in talking points
  • Stress-testing answers before they're on record
Do it yourself
  • Deciding what CN says on record
  • Confirming facts AI hasn't been given
  • Crafting apologies without Legal review
  • Anything about ongoing legal proceedings
  • Making commitments on timelines or costs
02

The hostile question generator.

Surface what the journalist is already thinking

Before any media interaction, run this prompt to find the questions you most need to be ready for. The goal is not to see them coming, it's to prepare an answer that will hold up.

You are a [beat] reporter at [outlet]. You've received a tip about [topic]. Generate the 10 hardest questions you would ask CN's spokesperson. For each: - The question (exact words you'd use on camera or in print) - Why it's hard (what assumption it probes, what weakness it exposes) - What answer would satisfy you enough to move on Rules: - At least 3 questions should be ones CN would rather not answer - At least 1 should be a "gotcha" that sounds reasonable but is a trap - Include 1 about what CN hasn't said yet - No softball questions
03

The 40-word statement pattern.

Short enough to read aloud. Tight enough to defend.

Forty words is not a constraint, it's a quality filter. If you can't say it in 40 words, you don't know what you're trying to say yet.

Draft a media statement about [topic]. Max 40 words. Structure (in this order): 1. Acknowledge what happened or what's being asked about (one sentence) 2. State CN's position or action (one sentence) 3. Bridge to what comes next (one phrase) Rules: - No speculation on cause - No commitment to timeline unless Legal has approved - Every word must be defensible in print - Read it aloud, if it sounds robotic, rewrite it - Do not use "at this time", "going forward", or "we take X seriously"
04

Red-team your statement.

Find the damage before the journalist does

Once you have a draft, run it through a hostile read. This catches the out-of-context quote, the implied admission, the missing fact, before they appear in print.

Read this media statement as a hostile journalist. Tell me: 1. The sentence you'd quote out of context for maximum damage 2. The follow-up question this statement practically begs for 3. The one thing it doesn't address that a journalist will notice 4. Whether it sounds like a human said it or a lawyer wrote it Statement: [paste]
05

Before & after.

The prompt makes the difference

Without a structured prompt

Prompt: "Write a media statement about the derailment."

Output: "CN takes the safety of our communities and employees very seriously. We are aware of the incident and are working closely with relevant authorities. Further updates will be provided as information becomes available."

Vague. Defensive. Zero information.

With topic, position, and constraints

Prompt: Structured prompt with 40-word limit, three-part structure, and explicit rule against speculation.

Output: "A freight train carrying non-hazardous goods derailed near Jasper at approximately 3am MT. No injuries reported. Our response team is on site and the corridor is expected to reopen by tomorrow morning."

Specific. Defensible. Actually useful.

06

Pre-send checklist.

Run this before anything goes out

  • Every fact in the statement can be verified right now
  • No cause speculation, not even "preliminary indications"
  • No commitment to a timeline unless Operations has confirmed it
  • Legal has reviewed it (or will review before release)
  • It reads naturally when spoken aloud
  • Under 50 words, if longer, find the sentence that can go
  • You have not used: "we take this seriously", "thoughts and prayers", "at this time"

Holding statements when the clock is ticking.

What AI is good for in a crisis, and exactly where to stop and call Legal.

For: Crisis comms Time: 5 min Sensitivity: high
01

The 60-minute drill.

What AI can do while you're waiting for facts

Great for
  • First holding statement (while you wait for facts)
  • Employee "we know, we're on it" note
  • Social media initial response
  • Generating the journalist questions that are coming
  • Drafting initial stakeholder notifications
Stop and do it yourself
  • Anything about cause or blame
  • Injury or fatality details
  • Regulatory commitments
  • Statements that admit liability
  • Anything Legal hasn't reviewed
02

Holding statement in 90 seconds.

For when you know something, but not everything

A holding statement does one job: buy time without saying anything you'll regret. Fill in only what you know for certain. Leave everything else blank.

Draft a holding statement. The situation: [describe what happened, what you know for certain, what you don't know yet]. Structure: 1. Acknowledge (what we know) 2. Show concern (if applicable, injuries, community impact) 3. State action (what CN is doing right now) 4. Promise update (when the next statement is coming) 5. Stop. Do not add anything else. Hard rules: - Under 40 words - No speculation on cause ("we are investigating" is fine) - No timeline promises unless Operations has confirmed - No "thoughts and prayers", show action, not sentiment - Use present tense for actions ("our team is on site") - Canadian spelling
03

The legal stop.

These conditions override everything else

The Legal Stop

If any of these are true, stop prompting and call Legal:

  • Someone was injured or killed
  • There is environmental contamination
  • A regulator (TSB, TC, Environment Canada) is involved
  • Media is reporting something CN has not confirmed
  • The statement could be read as admitting fault

You can use AI for the employee note and social post while Legal reviews the external statement. But the external statement does not go out without Legal sign-off.

04

Employee note & social post.

Two audiences. Two prompts. Both safe to draft now.

Employee note

This goes out before or simultaneously with the external statement. Employees should not hear about a crisis from the news.

Draft a brief internal note for CN employees. Situation: [describe]. Structure: One paragraph. What happened (as we know it), what we're doing, what employees should do (or not do, e.g., "refer media inquiries to Communications"). Under 80 words. Direct, not corporate. Rules: No speculation. No blame. Do not discuss cause. Canadian spelling.

Social post

First and only for now. Facts only. Give a time for the next update if you can keep it.

Draft a social media post (Twitter/X) about [situation]. Max 200 characters. Rules: Facts only. No speculation. Link to full statement if available. No "stay tuned", give a specific time for the next update if possible.
05

Before you release.

Nine checks. All of them matter.

  • Every fact can be confirmed right now by someone on site
  • No cause speculation, not "preliminary indications," not "appears to be"
  • No blame, not even implied
  • No timeline promise you can't keep (say "we will provide an update by [time]" only if you will)
  • Legal has seen the external statement
  • The employee note went out before or simultaneously with the external statement
  • Someone has checked social media for what's already out there
  • The statement reads naturally aloud in under 15 seconds
  • You have a named person (or role) for media to contact
Guides / Summarise a long document
Research & prep · 3 min read

Summarise a long document.

Three summary types, executive, skim, deep, and when to ask for each.

For: everyone Tool: ChatGPT + file upload Time: 5 min
01

Three levels of summary.

Pick the one that matches how it will be used

The prompt that gives you a summary you'll actually use is the one that names the level before it asks for anything else.

EXECExecutive.
3-5 bullets. Decision-ready. What changed, what it means, what to do next.
SKIMSkim.
One-page overview. Key themes, standout data, gaps. For briefing packs.
DEEPDeep.
Section-by-section. Strengths, weaknesses, what's missing. For SME review.
02

The prompt patterns.

One for each level

Executive summary

Upload the document first, then paste this prompt. The "so what" at the end of each bullet is what separates a useful summary from a table of contents.

Summarise this document in 3-5 bullet points for a senior executive. For each bullet: - Lead with what changed or what matters - Include one supporting number if available - End with the "so what", what this means for CN Do not include background the reader already knows. Do not pad with filler. If the document doesn't contain enough information to make 5 meaningful bullets, give fewer.

Skim summary

For briefing packs and prep reads. The gaps section is the part most people skip in their prompt, and it's usually the most useful output.

Write a one-page summary of this document for someone preparing a briefing pack. Structure: 1. One-sentence thesis (what this document is arguing or reporting) 2. Key themes (3-4, with one supporting detail each) 3. Notable data points (any numbers, dates, or findings worth highlighting) 4. Gaps or questions (what the document doesn't address that the reader might want) 5. Recommendation (if the document makes one, or note that it doesn't) Under 400 words.

Deep summary

For SME review and due diligence. The "weakest part" instruction forces AI to actually critique the document rather than summarise it charitably.

Analyse this document section by section. For each section: - What it covers (one sentence) - The strongest claim or finding - Any unsupported claims or missing evidence - One question a critical reader would ask End with: "The single most important thing in this document is..." and "The weakest part is..."
03

Upload, don't paste.

File upload preserves what copy-paste destroys

Pasting a document loses tables, headers, footnotes, and formatting. The file upload feature reads the document as it was meant to be read.

  • Click the paperclip or attachment icon in the ChatGPT message bar
  • Upload PDF, DOCX, PPTX, XLSX, or images directly
  • Better than pasting because formatting, tables, and headers are preserved
  • For documents over 100 pages, tell ChatGPT which sections matter most, or break into chunks
Before you upload

Check the confidentiality traffic-light guide before uploading any document. If a document is marked Confidential or Restricted, do not upload it to ChatGPT's public interface.

04

Before & after.

The level matters as much as the prompt

Vague prompt

Prompt: "Summarise this."

Output: Generic wall of text that restates the table of contents. Each section gets one sentence. No "so what." No prioritisation. You still have to read the whole document to decide what matters.

You could have skimmed the doc yourself in the same time.

Structured prompt

Prompt: "Exec summary, 5 bullets, each with a supporting number and a 'so what.'"

Output: Five tight bullets. Each leads with the finding, includes a number, and ends with what it means for CN. You know what matters before you open the document.

Decision-ready in 30 seconds.

05

The verification habit.

Summaries can be wrong. Here's how to check.

AI summarises confidently whether it is right or not. A summary that invents a number or conflates two claims is worse than no summary. Run these checks before using the output.

  • Does the summary mention anything not in the original document?
  • Are the numbers and dates from the summary actually in the source? (Check 2-3)
  • Did AI combine two separate claims into one? (Common hallucination)
  • Is the "so what" actually supported by the document, or did AI editorialise?
  • Did you read the sections AI flagged as most important?
  • If there's a recommendation, is it the document's recommendation or AI's?
Guides / Stakeholder & audience analysis
PAGA · Research · 4 min

Stakeholder & audience analysis.

Before a consultation, a minister meeting, or a community engagement, spend 10 minutes mapping who cares and why. AI accelerates the mapping. Your judgment stays in the loop.

For: PAGA, Government AffairsTime: 10 min
01

When this is worth doing.

And when it isn't

Every stakeholder engagement has a map. Usually it's in someone's head. Write it down once, and every subsequent meeting, letter, and talking-point doc gets easier.

✓ AI is great for this
  • Pre-meeting prep for MP, MPP, or minister engagements
  • Sketching out a consultation plan
  • Identifying who to brief first before an announcement
  • Anticipating opposition ahead of a regulatory filing
  • Tiering media outlets for a launch
✗ Do it yourself
  • Making commitments about what CN will do for any stakeholder
  • Guessing at what an individual stakeholder has privately said
  • Using AI to draft stakeholder-specific positions without Government Affairs review
02

The prompt pattern.

Map the field in one page

JOB: Map the stakeholders for the initiative below. Identify each stakeholder's likely position, the strongest argument for their position, and what they need from CN. Do not invent organizations or individuals, only include stakeholders I listed or clearly-named categories. AUDIENCE & OUTCOME: CN PAGA team preparing for engagement. They should come away knowing: who to brief first, who to brief last, and what each group will want to hear. FORMAT: Table with columns: Stakeholder | Influence (high / medium / low) | Likely position (support / neutral / oppose / unknown) | Their core concern | What they want from CN | Risk if ignored. End with a 3-bullet "who to brief in what order" recommendation. INFO: • Initiative: [describe the announcement, project, or ask] • Geography: [specific corridor, province, municipality] • Known stakeholders: [list the ones you already know of] • CN's primary objective: [what we need from this engagement] RULES: Canadian spelling. No em dashes. If you don't have enough information to place a stakeholder, mark them "unknown, needs Government Affairs input." Do not fabricate positions. Do not invent organization names.
03

The tiers that actually matter.

Four buckets, not fifteen

Don't let AI give you a 40-row table. Four tiers force prioritization.

TIER 1
Must brief before announcement. Direct relationship to the initiative. Will be first call a journalist makes if they're surprised.
TIER 2
Brief day-of, within hours. Will notice, but not likely to react first. Typically elected officials outside the directly-affected riding.
TIER 3
Inform via standard channels. Would want to know, won't feel slighted if they learn the same way as the public. Industry groups, most media.
TIER 4
Monitor for reaction. No pre-brief. Track their response for next steps.
04

Pressure-test with the inverse.

Who benefits from opposing us

The strongest map thinks about incentives, not just interests. Second prompt:

JOB: For the same initiative and stakeholder list, identify who has an incentive to oppose this publicly, even if privately indifferent. Why would opposing benefit them? What would they say? FORMAT: For each stakeholder with an opposition incentive: name, what they gain by opposing, the specific line they're likely to take, the first journalist they'd likely talk to. RULES: Canadian spelling. This is a red-team exercise. Don't soften. If no one has an opposition incentive, say so, that's useful.

This is how you build a Q&A pack that actually holds up. Most stakeholder maps fail because they assume goodwill.

Guides / Tone-shift
Editing · 3 min read

Translate tone across audiences.

The same message, rewritten for executives, front-line supervisors, unionized crews, and media, without losing what it actually says.

Works on: drafts already in handTime: 3 min
01

When to use this.

Cascading a single message

A service change, a policy update, an operational decision, one fact, many audiences. Writing four versions from scratch takes an hour. Asking AI to translate one well-drafted version takes three minutes.

✓ AI is great for this
  • Cascading one policy change across exec, management, FLS, crew, union notes
  • Turning a press release into an employee bulletin
  • Turning an all-hands script into a customer-facing post
  • Rewriting a briefing note as a media line
✗ Do it yourself
  • Translating news of layoffs, terminations, fatalities, or discipline
  • Shifting tone on anything going to regulators or Legal
  • Re-attributing quotes to a different spokesperson
  • Softening content Legal has already signed off on verbatim
02

The six CN tones.

Voice rungs

EXECS
Four sentences max. Numbers first. No preamble. End with the decision.
MANAGEMENT
Context, then what changed, then what they need to do. Plain, practical.
FLS
Direct. Short sentences. The words they'll say to their crews. No corporate softening.
UNIONIZED
Respectful, clear, no euphemisms. Explain why it matters to them. Never talk down.
MEDIA
Third-person. News-shaped: what, when, where, why, who. Every word defensible.
INVESTORS
Precise. Source every claim. Nothing unreleased. Nothing forward-looking without a disclaimer.
03

The prompt pattern.

One source, many voices

JOB: Translate the tone of the message below for the audience listed. Preserve every fact. Do not add new information. Do not soften or intensify, match CN's established voice for that audience. AUDIENCE & OUTCOME: [one of: executives / management / front-line supervisors / unionized employees / media / investors]. They should understand the message as well as the original audience would, without confusion about what changed. FORMAT: Same format as source, unless impractical. Preserve any direct quotes exactly. State word count target: [number]. SOURCE MESSAGE: """ [paste the approved, signed-off source message] """ AUDIENCE-SPECIFIC RULES: • Executives: 4 sentences max, numbers first, no preamble • Management: context → change → action • FLS: short sentences, practical, no euphemisms • Unionized: respectful, direct, explain why it matters to them • Media: third-person, news structure, every word defensible • Investors: source every claim, nothing unreleased, no forward-looking without disclaimer HOUSE RULES: Canadian spelling. No em dashes. No emoji. If a fact isn't in source, don't introduce it.
04

The test.

Read all versions side by side

  • Same facts in every version? (Scan dates, numbers, named people, named places.)
  • No version introduces a claim the source doesn't make.
  • No version softens a legally-relevant word. "Effective April 15" stays "effective April 15," not "starting around mid-April."
  • The FLS version sounds like a supervisor briefing a crew. If it sounds like a memo, redo.
  • The executive version has one sentence you could screenshot and post online.
  • The union version does not contain the words "empower," "align," or "synergies."
Guides / Memos & all-employee notes
Internal comms · 3 min read

Memos & all-employee notes.

Announcing a change without sounding like HR. The workhorse internal format, with the bits AI always gets wrong.

Audience: CN employeesTime: 10 min
01

When an internal memo is the right format.

And when something else is

Memos are for information that everyone needs and no one will ask for. If a manager can cover it in a team meeting, don't write a memo. If one group is affected, write them directly. Memos are for company-wide, non-urgent, factual changes.

✓ AI is great for this
  • Policy updates everyone needs to know
  • Structural changes ("X now reports to Y")
  • Milestones worth marking (quarter close, safety record)
  • Process rollouts with a clear start date
✗ Do it yourself
  • Condolences or grief
  • Legally binding terminations or discipline
  • Responses to breaking media stories (use a different vehicle)
  • Information that's actually confidential to a subset
02

The prompt pattern.

Built for an all-employee audience

JOB: Draft an all-employee memo. Do not use the phrases "excited to announce," "going forward," "moving forward," "at the end of the day," or "leveraging." Do not start with "As you may know." AUDIENCE & OUTCOME: All CN employees, from board administrators to conductors. They should finish reading knowing: what changed, when it takes effect, what it means for them, and where to go with questions. Nothing more. FORMAT: 220-280 words. Structure: 1. Subject line (under 8 words, states the change) 2. One-line opener (what changed, in plain language) 3. Context paragraph (why this matters, 2-3 sentences) 4. What it means for employees (bullets, 3 max) 5. What happens next (dates, milestones) 6. Closing line with who to contact INFO: • Change: [what] • Effective date: [when] • Scope: [who is affected, be specific] • Why now: [the honest reason] • Contact for questions: [name, role] • Background (for your eyes only, not for the memo): [anything sensitive the AI should avoid mentioning] RULES: Canadian spelling. No em dashes. No emoji. No exclamation marks. Spell out acronyms on first use. If a fact isn't in INFO, write [confirm], never invent. Do not speculate on related topics (cost, headcount impact, timeline beyond what INFO says).
03

The four edits you always make.

Before it goes to the CEO's office

  1. Cut the opening adjective.

    AI always opens with "I'm excited" / "I'm pleased" / "I'm thrilled." Delete. Start with the fact.

  2. Compress the "why now."

    AI writes three sentences of context. Usually one will do. Readers will scroll if you don't get to the point by line 3.

  3. Rewrite the "what it means for you" bullets.

    These are almost always vague in the AI draft. Name the specific thing a specific person has to do differently.

  4. Put a real name at the bottom.

    AI loves "contact your manager" or "reach out to HR." Name a person or a specific inbox. If you can't name one, you're not ready to send.

04

Pre-send checklist.

Before the all-staff list

  • The subject line states the change. It's not clickbait.
  • The first sentence could stand alone as a company-wide Slack post.
  • Every "[confirm]" placeholder has been filled or removed.
  • No em dashes. No "excited to announce." No emoji.
  • A named person (or a monitored inbox) is listed for questions.
  • The signatory has read it in their email client, not in a Google doc.
  • It's scheduled for a time employees will actually read it (not 6 PM Friday).
Guides / Briefing notes for meetings
Research · 4 min read

Briefing notes for meetings.

A one-pager that turns a 90-minute meeting into a 10-minute read. What to hold, what to concede, what to never say out loud.

For: executives, VPs, ministerial meetingsTime: 15 min
01

The anatomy of a useful brief.

Principal-ready in one page

A principal walking into a meeting does not need context. They need: what's at stake, what they're here to achieve, what the other side wants, and the three things they can't afford to say.

✓ AI is great for this
  • Compiling the participants list with roles
  • Surfacing positions from public statements, prior meetings, stakeholder letters
  • Drafting a first pass of talking points
  • Anticipating the three questions the principal will be asked
  • Structuring the page
✗ Do it yourself
  • Inferring what someone privately believes
  • Writing the principal's commitments without Government Affairs sign-off
  • Including confidential or speculative internal positioning
  • Pasting unredacted participant information (see the confidentiality guide)
02

The prompt pattern.

Built for a 10-minute pre-read

JOB: Draft a one-page meeting brief. Do not invent attendees' positions. Only reflect what is in the INFO below or explicitly marked as a known public position. AUDIENCE & OUTCOME: A CN senior executive reading this on the way into the meeting. They should know: who is in the room, what each person wants, our ask, likely traps, and three agreed lines. FORMAT: One page. Exact structure: 1. MEETING, title, date, 1-line purpose 2. ATTENDEES, name, role, affiliation, known relationship to CN (warm / neutral / critical) 3. WHAT THEY WANT, single sentence per attendee or bloc 4. WHAT CN WANTS, the ask, in one sentence 5. THREE LINES, three short statements the principal can use verbatim 6. TRAPS, two or three topics to steer away from, with why 7. ASKS, specific action the principal is seeking from the meeting INFO: • Meeting topic: [describe] • Participants (names, roles, affiliations): [list] • Any prior CN positions shared with them: [list] • Known public positions of attendees: [paste or link] • Known sensitivities: [what CN cannot commit to or discuss] • Outcome we're aiming for: [the ask] RULES: Canadian spelling. No em dashes. If you don't have enough information about an attendee, mark them "position unknown, Government Affairs to confirm." Never fabricate quotes or positions. Keep the whole document to one page.
03

The "three lines" discipline.

The most-used part of the brief

Principals rarely use talking points verbatim. They use lines. A line is a short, self-contained sentence that encapsulates a position and survives being quoted.

Three is the magic number. Two is too few (principal runs out and improvises). Four is too many (principal forgets or mixes them).

✗ A talking point

"CN is committed to supporting Canadian supply chains through ongoing investment in network capacity, safety, and the communities we operate in, while working closely with regulators and stakeholders to ensure a sustainable future."

35 words · unusable verbatim

✓ A line

"We move 300 million tonnes of Canadian goods a year. Safety is the reason we get to do it."

19 words · quotable, true, defensible

04

Pre-meeting handoff.

Before the principal sees it

  • Every attendee position is either sourced or flagged "unknown."
  • The "three lines" are pressure-tested: read them aloud. If any one sounds like a press release, rewrite.
  • The traps section names specific topics, not general themes. "Don't commit to a timeline on the Kamloops siding", not "be careful about commitments."
  • The ask is specific. "We want them to sign the letter by April 30", not "we want their support."
  • Government Affairs has reviewed anything involving elected officials.
  • It fits on one page in the principal's preferred reading font and size. Not on the assistant's screen.

Social posts.

The three CN voices on social.

Platform, not personality

The "sound human" test.

Before scheduling

Read the post out loud. If any of these phrases appear, rewrite.

✗ AI tells

"We're excited to announce..."

"Proud to partner with..."

"At CN, we believe..."

"Together, we can..."

"A testament to our commitment..."

"This milestone represents..."

✓ Replace with

Start with the fact: "CN is extending..."

Start with the partner's name.

Cut. Start with what we actually did.

Cut. Say what specifically was done.

Cut. The fact speaks for itself.

Start with the specific result: "300 new..."

04

The scheduling rules.

CN-specific

  • Check the day's news. Never post a celebratory LinkedIn during an ongoing incident.
  • Check the CN incident dashboard. If there's a service disruption, delay non-urgent posts.
  • Never schedule a post to X unless a live human is available to respond within 30 minutes. X replies age badly fast.
  • Do not schedule posts for Friday after 3 PM unless the content is time-sensitive. They sink.
  • Internal posts coordinate with HR and the internal comms calendar. Don't land on top of another team's announcement.
  • Any post mentioning specific customers or partners has been reviewed by Corporate Communications and the relationship owner.
