Writing · Most used · 4 min read

Draft an email in 90 seconds.

The workhorse guide. How to get a clear, appropriately-toned email on the first try, and the five edits you should always make before hitting send.

Audience: anyone Time: 3 min
Prompt Builder

Walks you through the Four Questions to build any email prompt.

Open tool
01

Use AI for this. Don't for that.

The first decision

AI is a first-draft engine, not a judgment engine. Use it for volume, structure, and tone. Don't outsource the decisions.

✓ Great for
  • Status updates, FYIs, chasers
  • Meeting requests and intros
  • Translating bullet points into prose
  • Softening or firming a tone
  • Writing in a language you're rusty in
  • "Say this nicely" rewrites
✗ Stop and write it yourself
  • Condolences or sympathy
  • Performance or HR conversations
  • Legally binding commitments
  • Anything involving a specific person's confidential situation
  • Apologies on behalf of the org, get them reviewed
02

What to have ready.

Copy, adapt, paste

Fill in the italic bits. Keep the structure. This pattern works in Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, or Gemini.

You are helping me draft an email. // Context I am [your role] at [org]. I am writing to [recipient, name, role, how well you know them]. Our relationship: [first contact / ongoing / tense / warm]. // Goal The single thing this email needs to achieve: [the ONE outcome]. // Inputs Here is what I want to say, in rough bullets: - [point 1] - [point 2] - [ask] // Constraints - Tone: [warm / professional / diplomatic-firm / apologetic] - Length: under [120] words - Do NOT use: "I hope this finds you well", "circle back", "touch base", exclamation marks - Match this voice sample from my past writing: [paste 2-3 of your previous emails] // Output Give me: 1. A subject line (under 8 words, specific) 2. The email body 3. One alternate version with a firmer tone Then stop. Do not add commentary.
Why the voice sample matters

Without it, every email comes out sounding like a LinkedIn post. Two or three of your past emails teach the model your rhythm, your sign-offs, your hedges. This single addition is the difference between usable and uncanny.

03

Before & after.

Vague prompt vs. the pattern

✗ Vague prompt

"Write an email to FLS about the schedule change."

Output (generic)

Dear Team,

I hope this message finds you well. I am writing to inform you about an upcoming change to the schedule that will be taking effect in the near future. As you may be aware, operational requirements have necessitated certain adjustments...

✓ Pattern prompt

Role, goal, bullets, tone "diplomatic-firm", 120 words, voice sample attached.

Output (usable first draft)

Subject: Yard reporting process changes, effective May 1

Starting May 1, all yard incident reports go through the new SafeTrack system instead of the paper form.

What you need to know:
• The paper form is retired. SafeTrack is the only accepted method after May 1.
• Training sessions run April 22 to 28 at each terminal. Your superintendent has the schedule.
• If you have questions before then, contact your local safety coordinator.

This change applies to all terminals in the Western region. Nothing else about the reporting process changes.

Stay safe,
[Your name]

04

Four variants to save.

For the situations you hit weekly

a. The "I disagree, politely"

When you need to push back without burning the relationship.

Rewrite this email so it: - Clearly registers that I disagree with [specific point] - Keeps the door open for next steps - Avoids passive-aggressive phrasing ("as previously discussed", "per my last email") - Ends with a concrete proposal, not a complaint Original: [paste]

b. The chaser

Second or third follow-up. The trick: acknowledge the silence rather than ignoring it.

Draft a follow-up email. This is my [second / third] chase. Rules: - Under 60 words - Acknowledge the silence briefly, without guilt-tripping - Restate the ask in one sentence - Offer an out ("if this has been deprioritised, just tell me") - No "gentle reminder", no "per my last email" Thread so far: [paste]

c. The stakeholder update

Monthly/quarterly note to a list. The risk: sounding like a newsletter no one asked for.

Write a stakeholder update email based on these bullets: [paste] Format: - Subject: what changed, not "Monthly update" - Opening line: the one thing they need to know - 3 bullet points, max 15 words each - One clear ask or next step - Sign-off: my name, nothing else Tone: peer-to-peer, not broadcast. Length: under 150 words. Cut ruthlessly.

d. The "thanks, but no"

Declining an invitation, request, or proposal without closing the door.

Draft a polite decline. Context: [who is asking, what they want, why I am saying no, our relationship]. Structure: 1. Thank them specifically (name the thing) 2. Say no clearly, no euphemisms ("unfortunately not" is fine; "at this time we are unable to accommodate" is not) 3. Give one honest reason if I have one; otherwise don't invent one 4. Offer something small if possible: a referral, a future window, an introduction 5. Close warmly Under 100 words.
05

The five-edit rule.

Before you hit send

Never send AI's first draft. Make these five edits, every time.

  1. Cut the opening pleasantry.

    "I hope this email finds you well" is AI's calling card. Delete it. Start with the actual point, or a one-line human opener ("Quick one before Thursday,").

  2. Verify every name, number, and date.

    AI makes up plausible specifics. If it says "the 2023 report found 62%," you haven't read that report, find it or remove the stat.

  3. Read it aloud. Cut anything you wouldn't say.

    "Leverage", "synergies", "circle back", "at this juncture", if you wouldn't say it to their face, cut it.

  4. Check the ask is in the first 3 sentences.

    AI buries the ask in the third paragraph. Move it up. Busy people skim.

  5. Add one specific, human touch.

    A reference to their last message. A remark about context only you two share. One sentence of you. This is the thing that keeps AI emails from feeling like AI emails.

06

Red-flag phrases.

Ctrl-F these before sending

If any of these survive in your draft, you sent AI's voice, not yours.

• "I hope this finds you well"
• "I wanted to reach out"
• "Please don't hesitate to"
• "In today's fast-paced world"
• "Leverage / synergies / align"
• "Delve into / dive deep"
• "It's worth noting that"
• "In conclusion,"
• "I would like to take this opportunity"
• "Moving forward,"
• "Crucial / vital / pivotal / robust"
• Any phrase that sounds corporate and hollow
Habit

Keep a "red-flag list" in a note. Every time you catch a new AI-ism in your own drafts, add it. Within a month you'll spot them in two seconds.

Editing & review · 3 min read

Proofread without losing your voice.

The trap: you ask AI to "polish" your draft and it hands back something that reads like a trade-journal editorial. Here's how to ask for corrections, not a rewrite.

Tool: any chat AI Time: 2 min
Review, Revise, and Adapt

Paste your draft. Pick your audience. Get a revision with tracked changes and coaching notes.

Open tool
01

Tell it how much to change.

This is the whole trick

AI defaults to the heaviest edit possible. Your job is to tell it how light to stay.

Fix errors only.
Typos, grammar, punctuation. Zero word changes otherwise. Use when you trust your own writing. The tool calls this "option 1."
Tighten the writing.
Errors plus clunky phrasing, repetition, and vague words. Preserves your voice. Use on first drafts. This is what you'll pick 90% of the time.
Restructure it.
Reorder paragraphs, cut sections, rewrite the opening. Use only when you know the draft is broken and need a fresh start.
02

What to have ready.

Before you open the tool

Proofread the text below. Do a Level 2 edit: CHANGE: - Typos, grammar, punctuation - Awkward or repetitive phrasing - Vague words where a specific one exists - Sentences that run longer than 30 words DO NOT CHANGE: - My vocabulary or sentence rhythm - Contractions (keep "don't", "we'll") - British / Canadian / Australian / American spelling, match what's already there - Anything that works, just to show you did something Return the edit as a tracked-changes list: → [original phrase] becomes [new phrase] -- because [reason] Do not return a rewritten document. I want to approve each change myself. Text: [paste]
The key move

Asking for a tracked-changes list instead of a rewritten draft changes everything. You see each suggestion, accept what works, reject the rest. You stay the author.

03

Before & after.

Voice intact

✗ "Polish this"

Original: "The meeting went sideways fast. We had three asks, got none of them, and left with a new problem."

After: "The meeting did not proceed as anticipated. Despite presenting three distinct requests, we were unable to secure agreement on any of them, and we departed with an additional challenge to address."

Your voice: gone

✓ "Tighten the writing" with tracked changes

Original: "The meeting went sideways fast. We had three asks, got none of them, and left with a new problem."

Suggested: → "sideways fast" works, keep
→ "got none of them" → "got none agreed" (clearer)
→ "a new problem" → "a fourth issue to handle" (specific)

Your voice: intact

04

Two special cases.

When tightening isn't enough

a. "Read this as my sharpest critic would."

Before sending something high-stakes, a board paper, a public statement, a letter to a regulator.

Read the text below as the most skeptical version of my audience: [e.g., a hostile journalist / an opposition MP / a regulator looking for gaps]. Give me: 1. Three sentences they would quote out of context to make us look bad. Quote them back to me. 2. Two claims they would demand evidence for. 3. One thing that's technically defensible but reads badly. 4. The single weakest paragraph. Do not rewrite anything. Just identify the risks. Text: [paste]

b. "Is this actually clear?"

For anything jargon-heavy, policy, technical briefs, legal-adjacent writing.

Read the text below and tell me: 1. What is this document actually asking the reader to do or believe? State it in one sentence, using the simplest possible words. 2. Which paragraphs, if removed, would NOT hurt that goal? 3. Which three sentences are the hardest to understand? Quote them back. 4. Is there a moment where the reader would reasonably get lost? Where? Do not rewrite. Just diagnose. Text: [paste]
05

Things AI should not touch.

Protect these in your prompt

  • Quoted speech. If you're quoting someone, AI will "tidy" the quote. Add: "Do not modify anything between quotation marks."
  • Names and titles. Add: "Do not change any proper nouns, titles, or honorifics."
  • Legal phrasing. "Without prejudice", "on a confidential basis", specific regulatory language, flag them in the prompt as do-not-touch.
  • Deliberate repetition. If you repeated a word for rhetorical effect, AI will "fix" it. Tell it the repetition is intentional.
  • Technical terms your audience expects. "Stakeholder capitalism", "material change", "in-camera", add a glossary of terms to preserve.

Writing · 6 min read · 4 templates

From bullets
to a speech you can actually say.

Written words and spoken words have different physics. AI knows written. Your job is to pull it toward spoken, short sentences, hard stops, and sentences your principal wouldn't stumble over.

Audience: writers, speechwriters, EAs Time: 10–30 min
Speaking Notes Generator

Asks about audience, topics, tone, and logistics, then drafts a speech you can actually deliver.

Open tool
01

Start with structure.

Pick a shape before you write a word

Most bad speeches fail at the outline, not the sentences. Ask AI for three outline options, pick one, then draft.

I need a speech outline. Give me THREE different structural options before I pick one. CONTEXT - Speaker: [name, role, speaking style if known] - Audience: [who, how many, what they care about, what they're sceptical of] - Occasion: [event, time of day, tone expected] - Length: [minutes] -- roughly [words] at speaking pace - The ONE thing the audience should remember tomorrow: [message] For each of the 3 options, give me: - The structural logic in one sentence (e.g. "problem → pivot → promise") - An opener (first 2 sentences, ready to speak) - 4-6 section headers with one line each - A closer (last 2 sentences) Do NOT draft the whole speech yet. Just the three outlines.
Why three

One outline is a suggestion; three is a choice. Seeing three side-by-side tells you what each structure costs, and usually makes the right one obvious.

02

Draft for the ear.

Different rules from writing for the eye

Once you've picked an outline, draft one section at a time. Long prompts get flabby outputs; short ones stay tight.

Draft the [section name] section of a speech for [speaker]. SPOKEN-WORD RULES (hard): - Average sentence: under 14 words. Cap at 22. - No semicolons. No dashes used as pauses. Use a full stop. - No words the speaker would pause before: "notwithstanding", "concomitant", "paradigm" - Read aloud test: if it needs a breath mid-sentence, cut it - Contractions: yes ("we're", "won't") - No lists of more than three. Three beats. Always three. - Repetition is fine. Repetition lands. Repetition is how speeches work. CONTENT TO WORK FROM [paste bullets, quotes, stats] Draft ~[N] words. Format with line breaks between paragraphs. At the end, flag any stat or quote I need to verify.
03

The opener & the closer.

Spend disproportionate time here

The first 30 seconds and the last 30 seconds are 80% of what the room remembers. Worth 10 prompts, not one.

Opener generator

Give me FIVE different openers for a speech by [speaker] to [audience] about [topic]. Try five different shapes: 1. A specific moment (a scene, a date, a person) 2. A direct acknowledgment of the room (why we're here, who's missing) 3. A surprising number or fact 4. A question the audience is already asking themselves 5. A short, honest admission Each opener: 2-3 sentences, under 40 words. No "Good morning and thank you for having me."

Closer generator

Give me THREE closers for the speech below. The audience should leave with one clear feeling and one clear action. One feeling: [e.g., urgency / pride / clarity] One action: [e.g., write to their MP / read the report / show up Thursday] Rules: - Under 60 words each - Last sentence must be speakable in one breath - No "in conclusion", "thank you for your time", "I'll now take questions" - Return to an image or phrase from the opener if one fits, but only if it fits Speech draft: [paste]
04

Anticipate the Q&A.

Before the speech, not after

If the speech will be followed by questions, and most are, rehearse them before you write the closer.

Act as three different people in the audience for this speech. For each, give me: - The single hardest question they would ask - Why it's hard (what assumption or weakness it probes) - A 40-word answer the speaker could actually give Three people: 1. A friendly journalist looking for a headline 2. A sceptic in the room who disagrees with the premise 3. A bureaucrat who wants to know about cost and timing Speech: [paste]
05

Pre-delivery checklist.

Final pass

  • Read it aloud. Not in your head. Out loud. Time it.
  • Mark the breath points. Slashes where the speaker pauses. If a sentence has no breath, rewrite it.
  • Cut 10% of words. On stage, every extra word is a drag. This is the single highest-leverage edit.
  • Verify every number, name, date, quote. A speech gets clipped on social media. One wrong stat lives forever.
  • Replace any word the speaker hasn't said in a real conversation this month. If it's not in their vocabulary, it won't sound like them.
  • Sound check for acronyms. Spell them out the first time. Don't make the audience decode.
  • Print it in the speaker's preferred format. Double-spaced, 14pt, bold the emphasis words. Paper, not phone.

Stakeholder relations · 5 min read

Letters: formal, firm, & human.

Letters are the slowest, most considered form of business correspondence. Tone is everything. Here's how to move a letter up and down the tone ladder without losing the point.

Stakeholder Letters

Eight letter types, from advocacy to condolences. Built for federal politicians and senior public servants.

Open tool
01

The tone ladder.

Same message, five registers

Before you prompt, decide which rung. Most "letter" prompts default to rung 3, which is neither warm enough for a constituent nor firm enough for a regulator.

Rung 1
"Warmly personal." Thank-you letters, condolences (only if hand-edited), invitations to people you know.
Rung 2
"Considerate official." Constituent replies, responses to public inquiries, appreciative stakeholder notes.
Rung 3
"Neutral official." Standard correspondence, confirmations, procedural notices.
Rung 4
"Diplomatic-firm." Disagreements, escalations, "your proposal is insufficient" letters.
Rung 5
"Formal-terse." Regulator-bound, legally-adjacent, pre-litigation. Run past counsel.
02

What to have ready.

Name the rung explicitly

Draft a formal letter. TONE RUNG: [1-5, see the ladder] -- If Rung 1/2: warm opening, acknowledge the person specifically -- If Rung 3: neutral, procedural, no sentiment -- If Rung 4: firm but not rude; register disagreement clearly; keep the door open -- If Rung 5: minimal; facts and positions only; no concessions beyond what I specify FROM: [your name, title, org] TO: [recipient name, title, org, full postal address] DATE: [today] RE: [subject line, specific] KEY POINTS to include (in this order if possible): 1. [point] 2. [point] 3. [explicit ask OR position] CONSTRAINTS - Length: under [one page / 400 words] - No clichés ("I hope this letter finds you well") - British / Canadian / US spelling: [pick] - If Rung 4/5: every claim must be directly supported by what I've given you. Do not infer or soften. Return the full letter including salutation, body, sign-off, and a placeholder line for signature.
03

Constituent & public replies.

Scale without feeling form-lettered

The trick: AI writes the body; you hand-write two sentences acknowledging their specific case.

Draft a reply to a constituent/member of the public. WHAT THEY WROTE (verbatim): [paste] OUR POSITION: [paste the policy/position note] RULES: - Rung 2 tone: considerate official - Start by naming the specific concern THEY raised (not a generic "thank you for writing about [topic]") - Address their ask directly, yes, no, partially, not yet, or "here's who can help" - If we can't do what they want, say why in one honest sentence. Not three paragraphs of process. - Close with one tangible next step (a link, a name, a date) - No "please don't hesitate to contact us", say how they can follow up specifically Leave a [INSERT PERSONAL NOTE HERE] placeholder before the sign-off for me to add 2 sentences by hand.
Never do this

Don't paste constituent data (names, addresses, case numbers, sensitive details) into a consumer AI tool. Redact to "[CONSTITUENT NAME]" and "[ADDRESS]", or use an approved enterprise tool.

04

The "diplomatic-firm" letter.

Rung 4, the hardest rung

The letter that records a disagreement, formally, without closing the relationship. AI tends to over-soften. You want clarity without contempt.

Draft a Rung-4 diplomatic-firm letter. SITUATION: [background] WHAT THEY DID / PROPOSED: [specifics] WHY IT'S UNACCEPTABLE (internal note, do NOT include verbatim): [candid] WHAT WE WANT INSTEAD: [specific ask] DEADLINE / NEXT STEP: [date and consequence] TONE RULES - Register the disagreement in the first paragraph, unambiguously. Do NOT bury it. - One paragraph of reasoning, fact-based, not emotional - The ask must be specific and time-bound - No veiled threats; state consequences plainly and only if I have authority to deliver them - Close courteously. "We look forward to your response by [date]." No warmth past that. Produce two versions: A) The one I asked for B) One clicks firmer (Rung 4.5), in case legal wants it pointier
05

Pre-mail checklist.

A letter goes on the record

  • Address correct? Name, title, honorific, organisation. AI gets these wrong constantly.
  • Dates and deadlines. Every date verified; every deadline achievable.
  • Position matches our actual position. Not AI's softened version of it.
  • Letters FOI/disclosure-safe. Would you be comfortable if this letter were made public tomorrow?
  • Signed off by the right person. AI doesn't know your delegation limits. You do.
  • Copied to the right people. CC line matches the politics.
  • Legal review if Rung 5. Non-negotiable.

Internal comms · 5 min read

30-page report to a 10-slide story.

AI is brilliant for the outline. Mediocre for the slides themselves. The trick is to stop at the right moment and take over.

Prompt Builder

Structure your deck outline step by step before filling slides.

Open tool
01

Outline first. Always.

Never ask for slides straight away

If you ask AI "make me a 10-slide deck", you get ten generic slides. If you ask for an outline, you get a spine you can defend.

I have source material for a presentation. BEFORE drafting slides, build me an outline. AUDIENCE: [who, what they already know, what they want from this meeting] LENGTH: [N] slides, [M] minutes ONE-SENTENCE GOAL: at the end of this meeting, the audience will [do / decide / understand]. SOURCE: [paste the report / briefing / bullets] Give me: 1. A one-sentence thesis for the whole deck 2. A slide-by-slide outline, for each slide: · a short title (under 8 words, stating the POINT, not the topic) · the single sentence that slide must prove · 1-3 supporting facts from the source 3. A list of anything in the source that I am NOT using, and why 4. The single slide the audience will remember, which one? why? Do not draft the slides themselves yet.
The "point, not topic" rule

"Q3 Results" is a topic. "Revenue grew 14% despite policy headwinds" is a point. Audiences remember points. They forget topics. Force this in every slide title.

02

Draft one slide at a time.

Resist batch mode

Once you've approved the outline, ask for one slide's content at a time. Slower, but the output is usable.

Draft the content for slide [N]. TITLE (from the outline): [paste] THIS SLIDE MUST PROVE: [the sentence] Give me: - A tightened version of the title (under 8 words, active verb if possible) - ONE big-number or big-quote I can place visually prominent, if the source supports one - 3 bullet points, max 12 words each, but only if bullets genuinely help. If a single sentence is stronger, return one sentence. - Speaker notes: ~50 words of what the presenter would say while this slide is up. Conversational, not scripted. - A note on what NOT to put on this slide (things the presenter should say out loud instead) Source for this slide: [paste the relevant section]
03

The density audit.

AI overfills slides. Trim.

Run this at the end, once the full deck exists.

Audit this slide deck for density. Go slide by slide. For each: 1. Count words on the slide (headline + bullets + labels, not speaker notes) 2. Flag any slide over 40 words as overloaded 3. Identify the weakest word on each slide, the one that adds the least 4. Identify any slide whose title is a topic instead of a point, rewrite as a point 5. Spot duplicate concepts across slides, two slides making the same argument Return a table with columns: slide #, word count, verdict (lean / okay / overloaded), one specific cut to make. Deck: [paste]
04

Visual suggestions.

What to put next to the words

AI can't design your slide. It can tell you what kind of image or chart each slide wants.

For each slide in this deck, suggest the visual. Pick from: - Big number (one huge figure, one label) - Chart (say what kind, what it shows, what shape it should have if the data supports the argument) - Photograph (say who/what is in it; a specific scene, not "people collaborating") - Pull quote (say who said it and why it belongs on this slide) - Diagram (describe the shape in 1 sentence) - Nothing, words only Rule: if a slide doesn't have a clear visual idea, that's a sign the slide doesn't have a clear point. Flag those. Deck: [paste]
05

Pre-show checklist.

Before you share screen

  • Every slide title is a point, not a topic.
  • Every number on a slide has a source. You know where each came from.
  • No slide over 40 words. If it is, split or cut.
  • You can present without the slides. If the slides disappeared, you could still make the argument. If you can't, you don't understand it yet.
  • Speaker notes read like speech. If they read like a memo, rewrite.
  • A backup slide exists for the hostile question. You know which question and where the slide is.
  • Final slide is an ask. Not "thank you". What you want the audience to do.

Q&A prep & media statements.

Surface the hostile questions before the journalist does. Draft a 40-word statement you can actually defend.

For: Media Relations Time: 15 min
PAGA GPT

Draft incident statements and Q&A prep for public affairs managers.

Open tool
01

Use AI for this. Don't for that.

Know the line before you start

Great for
  • Generating 20+ hostile questions in 60 seconds
  • Role-playing a journalist in back-and-forth
  • Drafting tight 40-word holding statements
  • Identifying weak spots in talking points
  • Stress-testing answers before they're on record
Do it yourself
  • Deciding what CN says on record
  • Confirming facts AI hasn't been given
  • Crafting apologies without Legal review
  • Anything about ongoing legal proceedings
  • Making commitments on timelines or costs
02

The hostile question generator.

Surface what the journalist is already thinking

Before any media interaction, run this prompt to find the questions you most need to be ready for. The goal is not to see them coming, it's to prepare an answer that will hold up.

You are a [beat] reporter at [outlet]. You've received a tip about [topic]. Generate the 10 hardest questions you would ask CN's spokesperson. For each: - The question (exact words you'd use on camera or in print) - Why it's hard (what assumption it probes, what weakness it exposes) - What answer would satisfy you enough to move on Rules: - At least 3 questions should be ones CN would rather not answer - At least 1 should be a "gotcha" that sounds reasonable but is a trap - Include 1 about what CN hasn't said yet - No softball questions
03

The 40-word statement pattern.

Short enough to read aloud. Tight enough to defend.

The verification habit.

  1. What is changing, exactly?

    The one-sentence, un-softened version.

  2. When does it take effect?

    A specific date, not "in the coming weeks."

  3. Who does this affect?

    Named groups, named yards, named roles.

  4. Why are we doing this?

    The honest reason, not the press-release reason.

  5. What happens if I don't comply / don't like it?

    The hardest one. Answer plainly.

  6. How will this be communicated to [union / customers / media]?

    People want to know they won't be surprised.

  7. Where do I go with questions?

    A named person or monitored inbox.

04

Before it ships.

The usual gates

  • Every numerical or date claim has been confirmed with the SME who owns it.
  • Legal has reviewed any answer touching on employment, discipline, regulation, or commitment.
  • The seven core questions above have real answers, not "more information to follow."
  • Nothing in the FAQ contradicts the all-employee memo or press release.
  • The document has a version number and a date. Future you will thank you.
  • It's shared in a controlled location (SharePoint, not open web) until the external version is approved.

The Four Questions, in depth.

Every guide in this library is the same four questions in a different disguise. Learn them once, and you'll never stare at a blank prompt box again.

Foundation Framework Works with any AI tool
01

Why a framework.

The case for rigour

The quality of AI output is almost entirely a function of the quality of the prompt. "Write an email about the service change" will always produce generic slop. A well-constructed prompt, specific about job, audience, format and information, produces a draft that's 70% of the way there.

The Four Questions is a simple structure that forces you to think through what you actually want before you ask for it. It works for ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, custom GPTs, and any tool that comes after them. It's not a gimmick. It's just the minimum brief you'd give a competent human writer.

Skim this page once. Come back to it whenever a prompt isn't working, almost every failure traces back to a question you didn't answer.

02

The four questions.

In the order you should answer them

1

What job am I asking AI to do?

Sets the verb · Sets the red lines

Pick one job. Don't stack them. An email is a draft; a press release is a draft; a fact-check is a fact-check. If you need two jobs, run two prompts.

  • Verbs that work: draft, rewrite, edit, shorten, expand, summarize, translate, brainstorm, critique, compare, extract.
  • Red lines: tell AI what NOT to do. "Do not speculate on cause." "Do not add a call to action." "Do not invent statistics."
Common trap "Can you help me with this email?" -- AI will happily help. With what, exactly? Pick a verb.
2

Who is it for, and what should it achieve?

Sets the tone · Sets the outcome

CN has six default audiences (executives, management, front-line supervisors, unionized employees, media, investors). Name the one you're writing for. Then say what success looks like.

  • Tone: direct, respectful, warm, firm, neutral, news-first, plainspoken. Pick one or two.
  • Outcome: what should the reader do, feel, understand, or decide after reading? If you can't say, you're not ready to write.
Common trap "For internal staff." That's 24,000 people across six functions. Be specific, who, doing what.
3

What format should it use?

Sets the deliverable · Sets the length

Be explicit. "Email, 150 words, subject line, three bullets, closing line" gives AI something to aim at. "Make it good" does not.

  • Formats: email, memo, press release, briefing note, Q&A, talking points, letter, social post, slide outline, statement.
  • Structure: give it the shape, headline + lede + quote, or context + decision + rationale + next steps.
  • Length: always give a word count or range. AI's default is too long.
Common trap Asking for a "short" anything. Short means different things to AI than to you. Give a number.
4

What information and rules does AI need?

Sets the facts · Sets the house rules

This is where most prompts fail. AI can't know CN facts that aren't in its training data and aren't in your prompt. Paste the source. Name the dates. Tell it what to do when a fact is missing.

  • Facts: dates, numbers, names, locations, decisions made, sources to cite.
  • House rules: Canadian spelling. No acronyms on first use. No em dashes. No emoji. Placeholder [confirm with SME] for missing facts.
  • Examples: paste a past email or release you want it to sound like. This is the fastest way to lock in voice.
Common trap Not telling AI what to do when it doesn't know something. Its default is to invent. Tell it to use a placeholder instead.
03

A worked example.

Four turns, from useless to usable

A supervisor needs to tell front-line crews about a new medical-evaluation process starting April 15 at the Montreal yard. Here's how the prompt evolves.

v1
The one-liner

write an email about the new medical evaluation process

AI produces 400 words of corporate-speak. Generic tone. Wrong audience (sounds like it's for managers). No dates, no yard named. References "our colleagues" four times.

✗ Unusable. Every fact is invented.
v2
+ Job & audience

JOB: Draft an email. AUDIENCE: Front-line supervisors at Montreal Taschereau yard. They pass this on to crews verbally. GOAL: They understand the change and can explain it without reading from a script.

Better tone, direct, practical. But still invents a process ("biennial review"), still no dates. Length untamed.

✗ Closer. Still inventing facts.
v3
+ Format

FORMAT: Email, 140-170 words. Subject line, one-line opener, three bullets (what, when, why), one closing line with who to contact.

Now the shape is right. Word count lands. Three bullets appear. But "contact" field is made up, and the "why" bullet speculates on safety rationale that isn't CN's actual reason.

✗ Shape correct. Content still untrustworthy.
v4
+ Info & rules

INFO: • Change: new medical-evaluation process per updated Transport Canada guidance • Starts: April 15, 2026 • Scope: Montreal Taschereau yard only (pilot) • Contact: [SUPERVISOR NAME, confirm with HR] • Reason: align with updated TC fitness-for-duty standard RULES: Canadian spelling. No acronyms. No em dashes. If a fact isn't in INFO, use [confirm with SME], do not invent.

Clean draft. Right length. Right tone. Facts correct. Placeholder where it should be. Three minutes of edits and it goes out.

✓ Usable. First real draft.

The lesson: each question adds precision. Skip one, and AI fills the gap with its best guess. That best guess is your problem, not its.

Literacy · Important · 4 min

It lies with confidence.

AI doesn't know what's true. It predicts what comes next, word by word. When reality runs thin, it keeps predicting, and writes things that sound right but aren't. Here's how to catch it.

Read before publishing
01

What it is.

A feature, not a bug

A "hallucination" is when AI produces confident-sounding text that is factually wrong. Invented statistics. Fake quotes. Non-existent people. Made-up regulations. Court cases that never happened. Case law with plausible citations that don't exist.

This isn't occasional. It's structural. AI models generate the most statistically-likely next word given the prompt, they don't check a database of facts. When you ask about something specific to CN, something recent, or something niche, the "most likely next word" path often leads somewhere plausible but fictional.

The tell: hallucinated output sounds more confident than real output. Real answers hedge. Hallucinations rarely do.

02

What AI will invent.

The usual suspects

  • Statistics about CN, our network, our peers, or anything market-sized.
  • Quotes from executives, politicians, or analysts, complete with attribution.
  • Dates of announcements, launches, incidents, regulatory changes.
  • Regulations, bylaws, and legal citations, with real-sounding section numbers.
  • Names of internal CN roles, teams, committees, and programs.
  • Historical events near the edge of its training data.
  • Sources, URLs, report titles, author names. The fake ones look exactly like the real ones.
The worst kind

Plausible invention. A fabricated statistic that's within the right order of magnitude. A fake quote that sounds like something the CEO would say. A made-up sub-regulation of a real Act. These are the ones that get past the first read.

03

The three-step habit.

That catches most of it

  1. Provide the facts. Don't ask for them.

    The #1 prevention: never ask AI to retrieve a CN fact. Paste the fact into the prompt. If you don't have it, find it first (not from AI). AI's job is shaping, not sourcing.

  2. Tell it what to do when it doesn't know.

    Add to your prompt: "If a fact isn't in INFO, use [confirm with SME], do not invent." This rewires AI's default. Instead of guessing, it flags.

  3. Challenge every specific claim before it leaves your desk.

    Ctrl-F for dates, names, numbers, quoted speech, regulations. For each, ask: "Where did this come from?" If the answer is "AI said so," it doesn't ship.

04

Red-flag phrases.

Stop and check

When you see these in an AI draft, treat everything around them as suspect until verified.

✗ Red-flag phrasing

"Studies show..."

"According to a 2024 report..."

"Industry data indicates..."

"A recent analysis found..."

"Experts agree..."

"As noted in the [plausible-sounding publication]..."

✓ What to do

Remove the claim, or...

Ask AI "Which study? Provide the exact title and author."

Then verify that source independently. Half the time it doesn't exist.

Never cite a source AI gave you without opening the primary document yourself.

CN rule

Any fact, number, date, or name that could be fact-checked by a journalist must have a non-AI source. AI can help you write the sentence, never source the claim.

Before you paste · 3 min read · Read this first

What NOT
to paste into AI.

The single highest-risk moment in your day. A traffic-light guide, green, amber, red, and a safe-redaction trick that lets you use AI on ~90% of the work you can't paste directly.

Assumption

You're using a consumer AI tool (the free or personal-paid version of ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot). If your org has an enterprise contract with data protections, the rules are looser, but check with IT, don't assume.

Green

Paste freely
  • Already-public press releases, speeches, reports
  • Your own first-person writing (emails you wrote, notes from your own head)
  • Published news articles and public policy documents
  • Prompts and templates (from this library or elsewhere)
  • Generic questions, "how do I structure a Q&A?"
  • Fictional examples you've made up to test a prompt

Amber

Redact first
  • Internal drafts not yet published (remove names, numbers)
  • Stakeholder correspondence (replace names with [STAKEHOLDER], orgs with [ORG])
  • Meeting minutes (redact attendees, specific statements, decisions)
  • Internal strategy that will be public eventually
  • Board papers (heavy redaction, or just don't)
  • Constituent letters (always redact names, addresses, case numbers)

Red

Never, no exceptions
  • Personal data (names + any identifier: address, DOB, health, finance)
  • Legal advice received, under privilege, or pending
  • Pre-announcement market-sensitive information
  • Security, safeguarding, or child-protection matters
  • HR matters involving specific people
  • Anything labelled confidential, restricted, or protective-marked
  • Whistleblower or investigation material
  • Login credentials, API keys, access tokens, ever
01

The redaction trick.

Unlocks ~90% of real work

Replace specifics with placeholders. The AI doesn't need to know who. It needs to know the shape.

Rd, Leeds LS11 8XY, case #C-2024-8831)
regarding her complaint of 14 March
about the air-quality monitoring at

breached 42 µg/m³ on 3 occasions

The key insight

The structure of your letter is the same whether it's to Priya or Patel. Let AI draft the structure. Fill the real names in yourself, in the safety of your own document.

02

Five redaction patterns.

Memorise these

  • People: [PERSON], [SENIOR OFFICIAL], [CONSTITUENT]. Include their role or relationship so tone is right.
  • Organisations: [COMPANY], [REGULATOR], [NGO]. Include the sector: "[COMPANY, a regional food processor]".
  • Numbers: round them, or replace with "[X]". "Revenues fell by [X]%" is enough.
  • Dates: "[DATE]" or relative ("last quarter"). Exact dates can tie to published events.
  • Identifiers: redact case numbers, NI numbers, account numbers, postcodes entirely. Do not paraphrase them.
03

When in doubt.

Three-question test

If you're hesitating, run these three questions. Any "no" means don't paste.

  1. Would I email this to a friend outside work?

    If the answer is no because of confidentiality, it's the same no for AI.

  2. Would I be comfortable if a screenshot of this prompt appeared in the news tomorrow?

    Not a rhetorical question. Consumer AI chats have leaked.

  3. If the person mentioned in this prompt saw it, would they object?

    If yes, redact until they wouldn't.

When to escalate, not AI

Anything involving safeguarding, credible threats, legal proceedings, or leaks, go to the relevant lead. Not AI. Not Slack. Not the group chat.

Literacy · Judgment · 3 min

When not to use AI.

Shorter list than you'd think. These are the situations where AI is actively the wrong tool, not slow, not fiddly, wrong.

01

Hard stops.

Close the chat window

Hard stop 01

Condolences and sympathy.

A derailment with fatalities. A colleague's loss. A community in grief. Do not draft in AI. Do not let AI near the words.

Why: authenticity is the message. AI flattens it.
Hard stop 02

Legally binding language.

Contracts, settlement statements, regulatory filings, anything Legal will review line-by-line. AI is not a lawyer.

Why: a single hallucinated clause is a problem.
Hard stop 03

Safety-critical instructions.

Operational bulletins, emergency procedures, evacuation guidance. These come from the safety team, verbatim.

Why: plausible-sounding wrong is catastrophic.
Hard stop 04

Market-sensitive material.

Anything about earnings, M&A, undisclosed projects, forward guidance. Do not even describe it in a prompt.

Why: confidentiality, selective disclosure, regulatory risk.
Hard stop 05

Real-time information.

Today's weather. Yesterday's service status. What a minister said this morning. AI's training data is months old.

Why: it will make up a current answer rather than say "I don't know."
Hard stop 06

Anything you can't verify.

If you don't know the subject well enough to spot an invented fact, don't publish the output. Pass it to someone who does.

Why: AI optimizes for plausibility, not accuracy.
02

The test.

Before you open ChatGPT

Would you send a polite-but-inexperienced intern to write this first draft? If yes, AI can help. If the answer is "absolutely not, this one needs judgment I can't delegate," close the chat window.

AI is a first-draft engine. Everything above is a last-draft situation, or a situation where a draft is the wrong starting point entirely.

Technique · 4 min read

Iterating: the follow-up is the skill.

One-shot prompts fail. The four-turn conversation that gets you where you need to go.

For: everyone Time: 4 min
01

Why one-shot fails.

The first response is raw material, not a deliverable

A single prompt almost never produces usable output. The first response is a rough draft, like handing a colleague a one-paragraph brief and expecting a finished product. The real skill is turns 2, 3, and 4.

Most people give up on AI after turn 1. They see output that's close but not right, decide AI isn't good enough, and go back to writing from scratch. What they're actually seeing is a first draft, and first drafts always need work, whether they come from AI or a colleague.

The difference is that with AI, the follow-up takes 10 seconds. You don't have to be diplomatic. You can say exactly what's wrong and what you want instead.

02

The four-turn pattern.

What to do at each step

  1. The brief.

    Give the Four Questions prompt. Get a rough first draft. Don't expect it to be right. Read it as raw material, not a finished product. Your job at this stage is to identify what's wrong, not to be impressed.

  2. The redirect.

    "This is too [formal/long/vague]. Fix [specific thing]. Keep [specific thing that works]." Be precise about what's wrong and what to protect. Vague complaints ("make it better") produce vague improvements. Specific complaints produce specific fixes.

  3. The sharpen.

    "Cut 20%. Move the ask to the first sentence. Replace [vague word] with [specific word]." This is where most output becomes usable. The redirect gets you to the right shape; the sharpen gets you to the right words.

  4. The final check.

    "Read this as [audience]. What would they object to? What's missing?" This catches blind spots before they cost you. Ask AI to inhabit the reader's perspective, not yours. You're too close to the draft by turn 4 to see it clearly.

03

Follow-up phrases that work.

Copy these. Adapt them. Use them.

The hardest part of iterating is knowing what to say. These phrases are specific enough to get results. Keep this page open while you're working.

"Too corporate."
Make it sound like I'd actually say this.
"Good structure."
Good structure, wrong tone. Shift to [audience].
"Keep 2 and 3."
Keep paragraphs 2 and 3. Rewrite the opener only.
"Shorter."
Under [N] words. Cut from the middle, not the ends.
"Now stress-test."
Read it as [hostile journalist / skeptical MP / tired employee]. What breaks?
"Three versions."
That's close. Give me three alternate versions of the second paragraph only.
"Wrong emphasis."
The facts are right but the emphasis is wrong. Lead with [X] instead of [Y].
"Sounds like AI."
This sounds like AI wrote it. Which phrases are the tell? Replace them.
04

Before & after.

Same brief. Four turns apart.

Turn 1 only

What you get: A generic, bloated email that opens with "I hope this email finds you well." Three paragraphs of context before the ask. Passive voice throughout. Closes with "please don't hesitate to reach out."

Technically correct. Sounds like it was written by no one. Will be half-read, if read at all.

Raw material. Not usable yet.

After Turn 4

What you get: The ask is in the first sentence. Two paragraphs of relevant context only. Active voice. Closes with a clear next step and a specific date.

Sounds like you wrote it. Reader knows what they're being asked before the end of line one.

Ready to send.

05

When to start over.

The problem might be the brief, not the follow-ups

Start over signal

If turn 3 still isn't working, the problem is the brief, not the follow-ups. Go back to Turn 1 with a better Four Questions prompt rather than piling on corrections. Three bad follow-ups won't fix a vague brief.

The signal is when every follow-up produces something that's different but still wrong. You're steering a ship that's pointing the wrong direction. The corrections aren't compounding, you're going in circles.

When that happens, stop. Open a new message. Rewrite the brief from scratch with what you now know about what you actually want. Turn 1 of a better brief will outperform turn 6 of a vague one.

06

Same chat, not new chat.

Context is the asset. Don't throw it away.

Follow-ups work because ChatGPT remembers the conversation. Every turn builds on everything before it. Starting a new chat loses all of that context, you're back to turn 1 with nothing carried forward.

Stay in the same thread for the full drafting session. The conversation is your working document. When you get to something close, you can say "we're nearly there, just one more change" and ChatGPT has everything it needs.

When to open a new chat

If you've gone in circles and can't recover the thread, open a new chat, but paste in your best draft so far, not the original prompt. You're not starting over; you're starting from your best work.

Build a reliable custom GPT.

A custom GPT is a ChatGPT configured for one job, locked to a specific set of documents, that produces the same shape of output every time. Three moves get you there.

For recurring tasks Team-shareable
01

What "good" looks like.

Two words

Reliability: same question in, same structure out.
Accuracy: uses approved documents first, cites them, and says "Not found in the sources" when the documents don't support an answer.

A custom GPT that fails either of these is worse than no custom GPT. It produces a confident-looking document with invented facts, at scale. Build for these two properties or don't build.

02

Three moves.

In order

  1. Define the job in one sentence.

    No custom GPT should try to do more than one thing. Write the single sentence that describes its job, including the audience.

    # Example job sentence "This GPT gives executives a sourced overview of CN's business, strategy, and performance, using only approved corporate documents."

    If the sentence has an "and" that could be split, split it into two custom GPTs.

  2. Lock the sources and require citations.

    Upload the approved documents. In the GPT's instructions, say this verbatim:

    "Use these as the primary sources. Any fact, number, date, KPI, or claim must be cited to one of these documents. If it cannot be cited, do not include it. Say 'Not found in the sources' instead of inventing an answer." Sources: • 2024 CN annual report (financial reporting) • 2025 investor fact book (network, KPIs) • CN investor presentation (current investor narrative) • Delivering responsibly 2024 (sustainability) • Management information circular 2025 (governance) Default assumption: unless the user specifies a period, use the most recent information reflected in the uploaded documents.

    The citation requirement is the safety net. Without it, the GPT pattern-matches from its general training data, and you lose accuracy.

  3. Standardize the output.

    Give it the exact template. Every output should look the same.

    Always use this template in this order: 1. Key takeaways (3 bullets) 2. Business overview (what CN does, network footprint, revenue mix) 3. Performance snapshot (top KPIs, year, currency) 4. Strategy and priorities (as described in investor materials) 5. Delivering responsibly (only what is supported by the sustainability report) 6. Sources used (bullet list, cite specific documents)

    Reliability comes from format consistency. Executives skim in the same pattern every time, give them that.

03

Test before you share.

Five questions that break bad GPTs

  • Ask it a question not in the documents. It should say "Not found in the sources." If it answers anyway, tighten the instructions.
  • Ask the same question twice in different chats. If the structure differs, tighten the template.
  • Ask a question that requires a number. Confirm the number is real, and the citation points to the right document.
  • Ask a leading question ("Why is CN the best operator in North America?"). It should pull sourced claims or decline. If it editorializes, tighten.
  • Ask about last week. It should say it only has the documents you loaded. If it pulls recent-sounding facts, tighten.
Governance

Before sharing a custom GPT with a team, make sure the uploaded documents are approved for the use. If one source is confidential, the whole GPT is confidential, treat it accordingly.

CN house rules for AI output.

Paste this block into any prompt. It's the Communications style guide translated into instructions AI can follow.

Copy-paste Works in any tool
01

The block.

Copy into every prompt

Drop this into the HOUSE RULES section of your prompt (see the Four Questions template). It takes ten seconds and changes the output more than anything else you'll do.

# CN HOUSE RULES, paste into every prompt • Use Canadian English spelling (colour, centre, organize). • Spell out acronyms on first use: "front-line supervisors (FLS)". • Do not use em dashes. Use commas, semicolons, or two sentences. • Do not use emoji. • Do not start a sentence with "I". Write in CN's voice, not a chatty voice. • No exclamation marks in formal content. • No hype words: "revolutionary", "game-changing", "unprecedented", "excited to". • Titles and role names: capitalize when attached to a name ("President Tracy Robinson"), lowercase when generic ("the president said"). • Dates: "April 15, 2026", not "15/04/26" or "April 15th". • Numbers: spell out one to nine, digits for 10+. Always use digits for stats, dollar figures, and dates. • If a fact isn't in INFO, use [confirm with SME], do not invent. Never fabricate quotes, statistics, dates, or names. • Every claim a journalist or auditor could challenge must be traceable to INFO. • Do not speculate on cause, restoration time, legal outcomes, or market-sensitive matters. • Do not use second-person "you" in public-facing news releases; use third-person.
02

Rule by rule.

Why each one matters

1

Canadian spelling

colour, centre, organize, specialize, programme only for broadcasting.

Most AI defaults to US English. CN is a Canadian company and writes like one. One word of American spelling in a minister's letter is the thing they'll notice.

2

No em dashes

Use commas, semicolons, or start a new sentence.

The em dash is AI's favourite piece of punctuation. Readers have learned to spot it. Removing it is the single biggest tell-reduction you can do.

3

Spell out acronyms

"Front-line supervisors (FLS)" on first use, then FLS thereafter.

Not every reader lives inside our org chart. Investors, journalists, MPs, suppliers don't know what PAGA or IOI or OTR means.

4

No emoji

Ever, in anything the company puts its name on.

Emoji are ambiguous, cross-cultural hazards, and they age badly. If the draft has a train emoji in it, it's not a CN document yet.

5

No hype words

Strike "revolutionary", "game-changing", "unprecedented", "excited to announce".

AI reaches for these. Readers skip them. Any sentence that still lands after you remove the adjective is a better sentence.

6

Placeholders, not inventions

[confirm with SME] beats a plausible-sounding lie every time.

This is the single most important rule. AI will invent dates, quotes, stats, and signatories unless you tell it not to. Tell it not to.

7

No speculation

Nothing about cause, restoration date, legal outcomes, or share-price impact.

Speculation in a CN statement becomes tomorrow's headline. "Investigation is underway. More information will follow." is always the right answer until it isn't.

8

Dates and numbers

"April 15, 2026." One to nine spelled, 10+ in digits. Always digits for money, stats, dates.

Consistent notation makes dates scannable. A release that mixes "April 15" and "15/04" looks like three people wrote it.

03

The pre-send scan.

Five things to search for

Before a draft leaves your desk, Ctrl-F for these. It takes thirty seconds and catches 90% of AI tells.

  • Search -- (em dash). Replace with commas or semicolons.
  • Search " - " (double-hyphen with spaces). Same treatment.
  • Search "excited", "thrilled", "delighted". If it's still there, it shouldn't be.
  • Search "color", "center", "organize". Swap for Canadian spellings.
  • Search "[confirm". Every placeholder filled or the document doesn't send.

I am a… Tell it who you are and who you speak for.

Writing to… Name them. Include relationship and what they already know.

The ONE thing this needs to do. One outcome. Not three.

Here's what I have. Bullets, source doc, voice samples, past examples.

What I want back. Length, structure, tone, what NOT to include.
