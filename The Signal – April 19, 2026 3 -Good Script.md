Getting people to actually read an email is getting tougher — and that's the tension we're looking at this week. Not just opening it. Not just skimming it. *Reading* it. Why's that so hard now? Because inboxes are packed, attention's fragmented, and a message can vanish in seconds if it doesn't feel relevant. We've all sent something important and wondered whether it landed. For us at CN, that matters a lot. Email still accounts for a large share of day-to-day communication — crisis updates, policy rollouts, deadline reminders, all of it. But it also means the standard's higher. If email's the anchor, every sentence has to earn its place — and nobody wants to write the message people delete before they finish the second line.

The attention problem is the first thing that stands out. PR Daily says internal communicators are producing more content than ever, while employees are paying less attention. Overflowing inboxes, constant alerts, and shorter focus windows mean even solid writing can get buried. So what helps? More visual formats, more dynamic layouts, maybe a short video, maybe a cleaner design that gives the eye somewhere to land. Does that mean every email needs to be flashy? No. It means attention must be *earned*. [pause] Not assumed.

Access is the next issue. Another PR Daily article looks at deskless colleagues — the people who aren't sitting at a computer all day. The author argues that a lot of internal comms miss the mark, not because the writing's weak, but because the audience never really sees it. If a message lives in a crowded inbox or doesn't work well on mobile, it's basically invisible. What's the fix? Less clutter, simpler structure, and formats that fit how people actually work. The message has to be easy to find, easy to scan, and easy to act on. If it isn't, it won't matter how polished it sounds. [emphasis] It has to be usable.

Then there's trust. Jessica Pantages, a corporate marketing VP quoted by PR Daily, says the real breakdown isn't only what leaders send — it's whether people understand it and believe it. That gap between leadership intent and employee understanding is where trust starts to slip. Wordy, corporate language makes that worse, especially during change. Her answer is to explain the reasoning more clearly, connect the message to what people deal with every day, and make room for dialogue. That could mean manager huddles, FAQs, or a follow-up conversation — not just another all-staff blast. Why broadcast when you could connect? [long pause] That's the shift.

AI fits into this, too. The MIT Sloan Management Review, a business journal, points out that AI can speed up repetitive writing — but the bigger value comes from learning. Draft with AI, sure, then ask what worked, what didn't, and whether people really understood the message. Did they act on it, or just click past it? If we capture those lessons, each round gets sharper. If we don't, we're just producing more volume. [pause] More isn't better.

So, what's the practical takeaway? Clarity, access, and trust matter more than clever phrasing. Before you send the next email, ask a simple question: does this make it obvious what matters, what's changing, and what someone should do next? If the answer's fuzzy, the email probably is too. And if it's too fuzzy, people will feel it immediately.

Here's a simple AI check you can try on your next draft. Write the email as usual, then paste it into ChatGPT or Copilot and ask two things: what are the three main takeaways in plain language, and what might confuse a frontline employee reading this on a phone? Compare that with what you meant to say. If the answers don't line up — or if the tool spots jargon, missing context, or unclear next steps — revise before you send. It only takes a few minutes, but it can catch the kind of gap that makes a message fall flat. [emphasis] Small effort. Big payoff.
